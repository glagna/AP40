Week 4 · Fluid, Electrolyte & Acid-Base Balance

May 4–10, 2026 · Last content week before the midterm

Last week we watched Santiago's kidneys fail and saw how filtration, reabsorption, and secretion work in concert. This week we slow down right there: how does the body decide what kind of urine to make, and why? Three different questions are being answered all the time. How much water is in me? (osmolality, ADH, thirst). How much sodium is in that water? (volume, RAAS, aldosterone, ANP). How acidic is my blood? (pH, the bicarbonate buffer, the lungs, the kidneys). Three questions, three sets of sensors, three timescales. Two patients carry the story this week: Reyna, a 52-year-old with diabetic nephropathy, and Oscar, a 40-year-old with treatment-resistant hypertension that turns out to be primary aldosteronism. By the end of the week, the urinary → fluid → acid-base story will live in your head as a single framework, and that framework is exactly what the midterm is going to test.

What You'll Learn
  • Describe the three body fluid compartments (ICF, interstitial, plasma) and predict water movement among them based on tonicity.
  • Distinguish how plasma osmolality is sensed (osmoreceptors → ADH + thirst) from how plasma volume is sensed (baroreceptors → RAAS, ANP, sympathetic).
  • Reason about ADH, aldosterone, and ANP as the three knobs of fluid and Na⁺ balance, and predict their effects on urine and blood.
  • Choose the right IV fluid (NS, D5W, LR) based on whether the deficit is volume, free water, or both, and explain why correction speed matters for the brain.
  • Use Henderson-Hasselbalch in plain language: pH depends on the ratio of HCO₃⁻ to PCO₂.
  • Classify any ABG into one of the four primary disturbances using a 30-second algorithm.
  • Calculate and interpret an anion gap, and use MUDPILES to narrow the cause of a high-AG metabolic acidosis.
  • Apply all of the above to two clinical cases (Reyna and Oscar): investigate symptoms, interpret labs, explain the cellular mechanism, and predict the trajectory under treatment.

Remember: Don't memorize equations or transporter names. Understand the logic of three sensors, three timescales, three regulatory axes.

Week 5 · Midterm Week

May 11–17, 2026 · Practice Monday · Exam Wednesday · No class Friday

Week 5 is the consolidation week. You are not learning new material; you are showing what you already know. Monday is a working session designed to expose your weak spots while there is still time to fix them. Wednesday is the midterm. There is no class on Friday. There is no ALW, no KC Quiz, no RCS Quiz, and no Application Activity due this week. Your time belongs to one thing.

What This Week Will Show

By Wednesday afternoon, you will be able to:

  • Trace any nutrient from ingestion through absorption to its final fate in a cell, naming the structures and transporters along the way.
  • Predict the body's response to a metabolic challenge (fasting, exercise, diabetes, hyperthyroidism) using the three stages of catabolism and the insulin/glucagon switch.
  • Reason about the kidney as the executive of body chemistry: predict GFR, urine concentration, blood pressure, and electrolyte balance under different physiological and pharmacological challenges.
  • Read any arterial blood gas in under a minute and use the four-cell matrix to classify it.
  • Apply all of the above to a clinical case: investigate symptoms, interpret labs, explain mechanism, and propose a treatment.

Remember: The exam rewards reasoning over recall. Show why, not just what.

Test-Day Rules: What You Can and Cannot Bring

These rules apply to both Monday's practice and Wednesday's midterm. None of them are arbitrary.

✓ Allowed: handwritten notes
  • Any number of pages. No page limit.
  • Must be in your own handwriting, on paper, prepared by you.

Why I want it this way. Writing notes by hand is the studying. When you write a concept out, you have to decide what is worth keeping and put it in your own words. That decision-making process is what builds the memory.

✗ Not allowed
  • Printouts of slides, assignments, study guides, or any material you did not write yourself.
  • Phones, smart watches, or any electronic device other than the Canvas browser tab (or this backup site, if Canvas is still down).
  • Internet browsing or windows beyond the exam page.
  • AI tools of any kind: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, browser-extension AI, or anything similar.
A specific note on AI

Some questions on this exam have been deliberately written to mislead large language models. If you paste a question into an AI tool, there is a high probability it returns a confidently wrong answer. Beyond academic integrity, AI use is a bad strategy on its own merits. Being caught using AI or any prohibited tool guarantees a zero on the exam.
